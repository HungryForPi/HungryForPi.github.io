Math contest things

I was a member of the NYC Math Team and the Stuyvesant High School math team, and am still involved with them. Among my activities as a math team student were writing short articles on various topics and writing problems for math contests.

Expository material

  • Notes on inversion, based on lessons taught by Mr. Kats.
  • A short review of Euclidean geometry, taught to the senior math team class at the beginning of the school year.
  • Some intermediate-level number theory, taught to the senior math team class at the beginning of the school year.
  • Notes on orders in number theory and their applications to contest problems. Featuring also Fermat's "Christmas theorem" on sums of two squares.
  • Notes on p-adic numbers, taught to the senior math team class during the spring semester. Prominently focuses on rigorously defining p-adic numbers as metric completions of \(\mathbb Q\) endowed with the metric \(|x - y|_p\) induced by the p-adic absolute value. Also features a few words on Hensel's lemma. Uses much content from the book on p-adic numbers by Gouvêa.
  • Notes on modular arithmetic and the Euclidean algorithm, taught to second-semester freshmen. Written with my friend Noam.
  • Notes on integer partitions for a two-week sequence of lectures for the junior math team. Featuring several proofs of Euler's pentagonal number theorem and a combinatorial proof of the Jacobi triple product identity. Perhaps my favorite set of lecture notes to date.
  • Notes on the construction of \(\mathbb R\) with Dedekind cuts, taught during a Mu Alpha Theta meeting.
  • Notes on permutation puzzles, taught during a Mu Alpha Theta meeting. Derives an algorithm to solve the 15-Puzzle. One should come away with a general idea of how to solve such puzzles, including even the much more complex Rubik's cube!
  • Notes on Schur's theorem, which says that if the positive integers are colored with finitely many colors, then there exist three positive integers \(x\), \(y\), \(z\), all of the same color, for which \(x+y=z\). Taught to the senior math team as a guest lecture in Fall 2024.
